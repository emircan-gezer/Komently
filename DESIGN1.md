The design style of rethinks.org is a classic example of Functional Minimalism (often referred to as Developer Minimalism or Academic Minimalism).

While it doesn't use trendy effects like Glassmorphism (frosted glass) or Soft UI (pillowy shadows), it adheres to a very specific aesthetic favored by engineers and researchers. Here are the specific design terms for what you are seeing:

1. Functional Minimalism
This is the core philosophy. Unlike "minimalism" used in fashion or lifestyle, functional minimalism prioritizes information density and speed. There are no heavy images, no tracking scripts, and no complex animations. The focus is entirely on the content (the "Cheat Sheets" and blog posts).

2. Typographic-First Design
The design is "content-driven," meaning the layout is dictated by the text rather than the text being squeezed into a layout.

Monospaced Accents: It likely uses monospaced fonts (like Courier or SF Mono) for dates and technical terms, which gives it a "code-like" or "terminal" aesthetic.

System Fonts: It often uses "System Sans-Serif" fonts (like Inter or Helvetica) which feel native to the user's device and load instantly.

3. "The Brutalist Lite" Aesthetic
While not as harsh as true Neubrutalism (which uses thick black borders and neon colors), it shares the same DNA:

Rawness: It doesn't hide the "web-ness" of the site. Links are clearly underlined or colored, and the grid is simple and honest.

High Contrast: It uses a "High Contrast" palette—usually pure white backgrounds with dark charcoal or black text—which is the hallmark of readability-focused design.

4. Card-Based Layout (Low-Fidelity)
The blog posts and cheat sheets are organized in a Card-Based Layout, but in its simplest form. There are no shadows or borders around the cards; instead, they use Whitespace (negative space) to separate sections. This is sometimes called "Border-less UI."

5. Tufte-Inspired Design
Named after Edward Tufte (a pioneer in data visualization), this style is common in academic circles. It avoids "chartjunk"—any decoration that doesn't convey data. On Rethinks.org, every line of CSS serves a purpose to help you read the math or code better.
